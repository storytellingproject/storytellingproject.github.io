WHO CONTROLS THE STORY?

⚠️ This website contains imagery and themes from The Last of Us. Some content may include depictions of violence, infected creatures, death, and emotional distress. Viewer discretion is advised.

CHAPTER I CONTROL

A World Already
Written

The world is already shaped before you arrive. Its events, characters, and direction exist independently of your presence. You enter a complete narrative where progression is predetermined. The story moves forward as intended, regardless of your input. This reflects a structured narrative where meaning is defined by the author (Fisher, 1984).

CHAPTER II PATH

Following a Set
Path

There is only one path, and it does not change for you.

You move forward within a fixed structure where progression is controlled. The design removes the possibility of deviation, creating the illusion of choice. You are not discovering the story, you are following an authored sequence. This limits player agency and reinforces authorial control over progression (Macdonald, lecture).

CHAPTER III EMOTION

Emotion By
Design

Emotion in this story is not accidental. Each pause, silence, and moment of intensity is constructed to guide your response. Your experience is shaped through authorial control rather than personal choice. Meaning is delivered through the narrative rather than created by the player (Todorov framework context).

CHAPTER IV CONFLICT

Danger By
Design

Conflict in this world is tightly controlled. Each encounter is placed to maintain pacing and narrative progression. You do not choose when danger occurs. The system determines when obstacles appear, reinforcing the structure of the story.

Challenge exists to support the narrative, not to alter it (interactive storytelling structure).

CHAPTER V ENDING

An Ending Already
Decided

The story reaches a fixed conclusion that does not change. Regardless of how you move through it, the outcome remains the same. The ending is predetermined and does not adapt to player input. This reflects a deterministic narrative where outcomes remain fixed regardless of interaction (interactive cinema example).

But what happens when nothing is written?

This shift moves from a fixed narrative structure to an open system where meaning is not pre-defined (Ryan, 2002).

CHAPTER I FREEDOM

Exploring Without a
Structure

You enter a world that has no predefined structure or narrative direction. There are no events waiting and no story prepared in advance. Nothing begins until you decide to act. The world exists as an open system without guidance or expectation. This reflects a non-linear narrative where meaning is not predetermined (Ryan, 2002).

CHAPTER II CHOICE

Choosing Your
Own Direction

There is no fixed direction and no correct way to begin. You decide where to go, what to explore, and what matters. Each decision leads to a different outcome, shaping the experience through your actions. The story begins through player interaction. This expands player agency, shifting control from author to player (Macdonald, lecture).

Your story is different every single time you play.

CHAPTER III CREATION

Creating Meaning Through
Action

Nothing in this world carries meaning on its own. What you build, explore, and prioritise becomes the story itself. Actions generate meaning rather than follow it. This reflects emergent narrative, where the story is created through gameplay rather than predefined structure (interactive storytelling theory).

CHAPTER IV EXPLORATION

Exploring Without a
Structure

There is no required sequence and no enforced progression. You move freely through the world, encountering moments based on your decisions. Experience is shaped through exploration rather than design. This aligns with open systems where narrative develops through interaction rather than linear progression (Ryan, 2002).

No two Minecraft worlds are ever the same.

CHAPTER V CONTINUATION

A Story Without
an Ending

There is no final scene and no fixed conclusion that brings everything together. The story continues as long as you remain within the world, shaped entirely by your choices and actions. This removes narrative closure, allowing continuous player-driven storytelling rather than a deterministic outcome (interactive narrative theory).

FINAL REFLECTION

How Do You Want To Experience A Story?

You have explored two different approaches to storytelling. One follows a fixed narrative where structure, pacing, and meaning are controlled by the author. The other presents an open system where meaning is shaped through player interaction. One limits player agency, while the other expands it. This reflects a key tension in digital storytelling between authorial control and player freedom (Fisher, 1984; Ryan, 2002; Macdonald, lecture).

The Last of Us Authored, cinematic, and emotionally controlled. A fixed path where meaning is carefully shaped by the story itself.
Minecraft Open, interactive, and shaped by player action. A flexible world where meaning emerges through choice and exploration.

How to navigate

Welcome

This is an interactive digital story comparing two types of storytelling experiences. You can explore it any way you like but starting with "Follow a controlled story" on the home screen gives you the full intended journey.

Arrow keys and scroll

Use your keyboard arrow keys (← →) or scroll the mouse wheel to move between slides.

Bottom dots

There is a row of dots at the bottom shows where you are. Hover a dot to see its name, then click it to jump directly to any slide.

Home

Top Buttons

The top-right buttons jump instantly between the two game's storytelling sections. "Home" on the top-left always returns you to the very first page.

🔊

Audio toggle

The 🔇 button at the bottom-right toggles background audio for each slide. Click once to unmute, click again to mute. First and last slide dont have sound :)
